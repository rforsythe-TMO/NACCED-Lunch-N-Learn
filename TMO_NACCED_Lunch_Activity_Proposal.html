1.1

Team Sizing & Table Configuration

Tables are teams. Aim for 6–8 people per table. That's the range where everyone can contribute in the 30–60 second discussion windows, the captain coordinates rather than dictates, and quieter voices still have room. It also matches the standard banquet round configuration most hotels set by default. Below 5 per table and discussion feels thin; above 9 and people start checking out.

Headcount-to-Table Mapping

60–80 attendees
Tables of 6 → 10–13 teams. Ideal range for both formats.
80–120 attendees
Tables of 6–8 → 10–20 teams. Either table size works. Fewer teams = easier scoring logistics.
120–180 attendees
Tables of 8 → 15–22 teams. Keep it under ~22 teams so runners can scan paddles and the leaderboard stays meaningful.
180+ attendees
Tables of 8, capped at ~25 teams. Beyond that, consider capping or splitting rooms.

Uneven tables are fine — scoring is per-table, not per-person. 95 attendees? Do 11 tables of 8 and 1 table of 7.

What we need from NACCED

Estimated attendance for the lunch specifically (not the whole conference — some people will skip), and the venue's default banquet round size. If the hotel sets 10-tops, seat 8 per table and leave 2 empty chairs for comfort.

The Funding Feud

2.1

How the Game Works — The Big Picture

Family Feud is one of the most recognizable game shows in television history, running since 1976. The premise is simple: two teams compete to guess the most popular answers to survey questions. A host reads a question like "Name something people forget to bring on vacation," and the teams race to guess what the most common survey responses were. The top answer earns the most points, the second-most-popular answer earns fewer, and so on down the board. If a team can't guess the remaining answers, the other team gets a chance to "steal" all the points with a single correct guess. The show's energy comes from the tension between what you think people said and what they actually said — and from the theatrical reveal of each answer on the board.

Our adaptation

Instead of celebrity families competing on a TV set, every table in the room is a team competing simultaneously. Instead of survey answers from random strangers, the answers come from community development professionals — both from a pre-event survey of NACCED members and TMO clients, and from live polling in the room during the first 10 minutes. Attendees scan a QR code, answer questions about their own work, and those responses become the game's answer board. When the emcee reveals the top answers, attendees are seeing what their own peers — including people sitting right next to them — actually said. That "wait, here's what I picked — what did you say?" moment is what makes this format land. Tables hold up dry-erase paddles with their guesses, and the closer their guess is to the most popular answer, the more points they earn. The game builds to a championship finale called "Fast Cash for Community" — the top two tables send players to the stage for a rapid-fire head-to-head round, competing for a charitable donation in their organization's name.

A Family Feud–style game where tables compete to guess the most common answers from a survey of community development professionals — collected live in the room during the opening 10 minutes via a mobile QR poll, with a pre-event survey running in parallel as internal preparation. The displayed answer board reflects what the room itself said.

2.2

Game Mechanics

Every table is a team. Each table elects a captain who submits the official paddle answer.

The handoff

Between 0:10–0:20, the live poll captures the room's answers to all 5–6 game questions in sequence. By round 1 (0:25), the producer has finalized top-5 answer boards ready to reveal. Attendees see the response visual fill in real time during polling, but final top-5 boards aren't shown until each round's reveal.

A Standard Round

  1. Emcee reads the question: "Earlier you all told us… let's see what your room said."
  2. Tables get 60 seconds to discuss and write what they think the top answer was
  3. All tables hold up paddles simultaneously — runners and producer scan and confirm matches
  4. Emcee reveals the room's #1 first (10 pts), then #2 (8) #3 (6) #4 (4) #5 (2)
  5. "Field Insight" spot prize for the most creative unranked answer — keeps energy up
  6. Emcee delivers a 30-second insight per round, comparing the room's data to the pre-event national data — this is where TMO's field-wide perspective gets earned

Pacing rule: No round runs over 8 minutes. Producer signals emcee at the 6-minute mark.

2.4

The Steal Mechanic · Two Options for Internal Decision

A core feature of Family Feud is the steal: when one team can't get all the answers, the other team gets a chance. In a multi-table format with 20+ teams, "give the points to the other team" doesn't have an obvious target, so we've designed two adaptations. Both preserve the steal's strategic purpose — reward attention, punish missing the obvious.

Common trigger

The steal activates only if 2 or more of the top-5 answers go unrevealed after the main reveal phase. Rounds where the room nails the top answers feel different from rounds where they miss — emcee simply says "no steal needed, you got them all" and moves on. Unpredictability is part of the fun.

Option A · The Wildcard Steal Recommended

How it works
When triggered, all tables get 30 seconds to write down ONE additional guess. Any table that names a remaining unrevealed answer earns 5 bonus points. Multiple tables can score on the same steal.
Pros
Every table participates, not just one. Fast. Rewards tables who were close in the main round. Creates a second wave of energy mid-round.
Cons
Less dramatic than head-to-head. "Stealing" feeling is softer because the room isn't taking from a specific opponent.
Tone
"Did your table catch a wildcard?" Collegial.

Option B · Steal from the Leader Alternate

How it works
When triggered, the current 1st-place table sits out. All other tables get 30 seconds to submit a guess. Correct guesses score double points (10) AND siphon 5 points from the leader's total.
Pros
Dramatic. Real underdog moment. Closest to the original show — points actually move between teams.
Cons
Punishes the table playing well. Can feel arbitrary to county staff who take competition seriously. Same leader round after round means the same table keeps getting targeted.
Tone
"The leaders are vulnerable." Competitive.
Decision lens

Given this audience — county government professionals, peer environment, mission-driven — Option A is likely the safer and more on-brand choice. Option B would create more memorable highs and lows if the room embraces the competition. Worth a quick gut check with NACCED on what will land best with their members.

Spin the Block

"Spin the Block" is a play on Community Development Block Grants — an inside joke this audience will get immediately. The game is inspired by the TV show Wheel of Fortune, adapted so every table plays every round.

3.1

What Is Wheel of Fortune?

Wheel of Fortune has been on TV since 1975. There's a large spinning wheel and a puzzle board showing a hidden phrase — like a hangman board where you can see how many words and letters there are, but all the letters are blank. Contestants take turns spinning the wheel and guessing letters. If a letter is in the puzzle, it appears on the board. You keep guessing until you think you know the phrase, then you try to solve. The fun comes from watching the phrase slowly appear letter by letter, the excitement of the wheel spin, and the tension of deciding when to solve.

3.2

How We Adapted It for NACCED

The TV show has 3 contestants taking turns. We have a room full of tables. So we changed the format: every table plays every puzzle at the same time, looking at the same shared screen. The puzzle board is projected on the big screen for the whole room. Letters are revealed one at a time — everyone sees every letter as it appears. There's no hidden information between tables.

The core game

The game is about when you choose to solve. Every table sees the same puzzle on the same shared screen. Letters are revealed one at a time by the emcee, with 4 pauses per round where tables can attempt to solve. Solve early with fewer letters visible and you keep more of your points. Wait for more letters and you'll be more confident, but you'll earn less. Two wheels add variety: a big wheel at the front of the room sets the round's rules for everyone (e.g., "Speed Round," "Double Points," "Blind Round"), and a table spinner at each table determines your starting point bank — so every table has different stakes. Each table gets 2 solve attempts per round. The whole game is one decision at each pause: "Do we know enough to solve right now, or do we wait?"

Field expertise is directly rewarded. A table full of CDBG veterans will recognize "ENVIRONMENTAL REVIEW" after seeing just E, N, and V — and solve at Pause 1 for maximum points. A newer team might need 8 or 9 letters to be sure, solving later for fewer points. Meanwhile, the big wheel might have made this a "Double Points" round, and your table spinner might have given you 120 points instead of 80 — so the stakes and rewards are different at every table, every round.

3.3

Two Wheels — How They Work Together

Every round has two spins: a big wheel at the front of the room that sets the rules for everyone, and a small table spinner that every captain spins at their own table.

The Big Wheel Front of Room

What it does
Sets the round rules that apply to every table equally. Modifiers change the pace, difficulty, or scoring of each round — for example, letters might come faster, points might be doubled, or the category might be hidden until letters start appearing.
Who spins it
One captain per round, rotating. The emcee calls up a different table captain each round — a quick spotlight moment.
Physical or digital?
Digital (projected on screen) for main rounds. Physical wheel for the championship finale.

The Table Spinner Every Table

What it does
Sets your table's starting point bank for the round. Different tables start with different amounts. "What are our stakes?"
Who spins it
Every captain spins at their own table, simultaneously. The whole room spins at once — you hear cheers and groans as results land.
Physical or digital?
Small branded tabletop spinner (cardboard + plastic arrow, TMO-branded). Doubles as take-home swag.
3.4

What's on the Big Wheel? — Room Modifiers

16 slots. Each one changes something about how the round plays for every table in the room.

Standard Round × 3
No special rules. Letters revealed at normal pace. Gives the room a baseline.
Speed Round × 2
Letters revealed faster than normal. Less time to think between pauses. Creates urgency.
Generous Round × 2
The emcee pre-reveals 3 free letters before the round begins. Everyone gets a head start.
Tough Round × 1
The emcee starts by revealing uncommon letters instead of common ones. The puzzle stays harder longer.
Blind Round × 1
No letters revealed for the first 30 seconds. Tables can try to solve from the category and puzzle shape alone for huge points.
Countdown Round × 2
Tables only get 2 solve attempts instead of the normal amount. Higher pressure, choose wisely.
Best Practice Round × 1
After the solve, every table writes a one-sentence best practice related to the puzzle phrase. Emcee picks the sharpest one for a spot prize.
Audience Choice × 1
Emcee offers 3 category options. The room votes by applause. The crowd picks the round.
Mystery Round × 1
The category isn't revealed until after the first 3 letters appear. Tables guess without context at first.
Bankrupt × 1
All points earned this round are halved for every table. Rare, dramatic, keeps the leaderboard tight.
Double Points × 1
All points earned this round are doubled for every table. The big swing round.
3.5

What's on the Table Spinner? — Your Starting Point Bank

16 slots. Most determine how many points your table starts with for the round. A few add special abilities. Every captain spins at the same time — you hear the whole room react.

100 Points × 4
Standard starting bank. Solid foundation.
120 Points × 2
Larger bank. You can afford to wait a little longer and still come out ahead.
80 Points × 2
Tighter bank. Pressure to solve earlier or accept lower points.
150 Points × 1
The jackpot. Tons of room to play with.
60 Points × 1
Tiny bank. You're almost forced to swing early. The groan from the table is part of the fun.
Double or Nothing × 1
No bank at all. Instead: solve correctly = 200 points flat. Don't solve = 0. High drama.
Free Clue + 100 × 1
100-point bank plus your captain can privately ask the emcee (via a runner) whether one specific letter is in the puzzle before the round starts. One free peek.
Insurance + 100 × 1
100-point bank plus your first wrong solve attempt doesn't count against your 2-guess limit. Effectively gives your table 3 attempts instead of 2.
Extra Guess + 100 × 1
100-point bank plus you get one additional solve attempt beyond the normal limit.
No Effect × 2
100-point bank, no special ability. Straight play.
3.6

What a Round Actually Looks Like — Step by Step

Here's exactly what happens in one round, from start to finish. Imagine you're sitting at a table of 7 people.

Step 1

The big wheel spins

The emcee calls up a table captain: "Table 4, come spin for us!" The captain walks to the front, spins the big wheel. It lands on "Speed Round." The emcee announces: "Letters are coming fast this round — stay sharp!" Everyone now knows the rules for this round.

Step 2

Every table spins their table spinner

The emcee says: "Captains, spin your tables!" All captains spin their tabletop spinners simultaneously. Your table lands on 120 Points — cheers. The table next to you lands on 60 Points — groans. Another table hits Double or Nothing — gasps. Every table now knows their personal stakes for this round.

Step 3

The category and puzzle appear

The emcee announces: "This round's category is Project Roadblocks." On the big screen, the puzzle board shows blank squares: _ _ _ _ _ _ _ _ _ _ _ _ _ _ _ _ _ _ _. Two words. 13 letters and 6 letters. Everyone sees the same thing.

Step 4

Letters start appearing — Pause 1

The emcee reveals 3 letters, one at a time. Everyone sees them on the shared screen: E appears in positions 1, 5, and 14. N appears in positions 2 and 8. The board now shows: E N _ _ E _ _ _ N _ _ _ _ _ E _ _ _ _. The emcee pauses: "Does anyone want to solve?" Your table huddles: "Project Roadblocks, E and N showing up a lot… could be ENVIRONMENTAL REVIEW." You have 120 points in your bank right now. Do you solve? Or wait for more letters to be sure?

Step 5

Tables decide — solve or wait

Your table is pretty confident. Your captain writes "ENVIRONMENTAL REVIEW" on the paddle and holds it up. A runner comes by, checks it, gives a thumbs up. You solved at Pause 1 — you keep your full 120-point bank plus a 25-point solve bonus = 145 points this round. Three other tables also solved at Pause 1. Seven tables decided to wait.

Step 6

More letters — Pause 2

For the tables that waited, the emcee reveals 2–3 more letters. The board fills in further. Their banks drop from 100 to 75 (or from 120 to 90, etc.). Some solve now. Some wait again.

Step 7

Pauses 3 and 4

More letters each time. Banks keep shrinking. By Pause 4, most of the puzzle is visible and almost every table should be able to solve — but they're earning fewer points. Tables that still haven't solved and are out of guesses earn 0 for the round.

Step 8

The reveal and scoring

The emcee reveals the full answer on the big screen. Announces which tables solved at each pause, their point totals. Updates the leaderboard. Delivers a 30-second insight about the puzzle topic. Hands out a "Sharpest Solve" spot prize to the table that solved earliest or most creatively.

3.7

Scoring — How Points Work

Two things determine your score each round: when you solve and what your table spinner gave you.

YOUR BANK

Point bank shrinks each pause

  • Pause 1 (3 letters): Keep 100% of your bank
  • Pause 2 (5–6 letters): Keep 75% of your bank
  • Pause 3 (7–8 letters): Keep 50% of your bank
  • Pause 4 (9+ letters): Keep 25% of your bank
  • Never solve: 0 points
SOLVE BONUS

Flat bonus for solving

  • Every correct solve: +25 points regardless of when
  • This means even a Pause 4 solve earns something meaningful
  • A table with a 100-point bank that solves at Pause 4: 25 + 25 = 50 points
EXAMPLE

Same round, different outcomes

  • Table A (bank: 120, solves Pause 1): 120 + 25 = 145 pts
  • Table B (bank: 100, solves Pause 2): 75 + 25 = 100 pts
  • Table C (bank: 60, solves Pause 1): 60 + 25 = 85 pts
  • Table D (bank: 100, solves Pause 4): 25 + 25 = 50 pts
  • Table E (Double or Nothing, solves): 200 pts
  • Table F (bank: 150, never solves): 0 pts
Wrong guesses

Each table gets 2 solve attempts per round. A wrong guess doesn't lose you points — but it burns one of your attempts. If you use both and get both wrong, you're done for the round and earn 0. This prevents teams from just guessing at every pause, and creates a real cost to guessing before you're ready.

3.12

Risks & Contingencies

Tables don't understand the "bank shrinks each pause" mechanic
The practice round is critical — run one full unscored round so everyone sees the bank drop in action. The emcee explicitly narrates: "If you solve now, you keep 100. If you wait, it drops to 75."
One table solves early and shouts the answer
Answers are written on paddles, not spoken. Runners check silently. The emcee doesn't confirm any solve until the round ends. Shouting an answer = that table's solve is disqualified.
Physical wheel doesn't arrive or breaks
Digital wheel fallback for championship round. Emcee plays it off naturally.
Table spinners land on the same result and feel unfair
With 16 slots and 10–20 tables, overlaps are expected and fine. A few tables with the same bank doesn't feel unfair — it's the table-specific variation that matters, not uniqueness.
Puzzles too easy — everyone solves at Pause 1
Pre-test with TMO clients. Difficulty should ramp across rounds. Use longer or less common phrases in later rounds.
Puzzles too hard — nobody solves before Pause 4
Emcee has backup easier puzzles. Can also add a mid-round hint ("Think about the Environmental Review process…") to keep the room moving.
Runners can't check all paddles fast enough at each pause
Only tables attempting to solve hold up paddles — not every table every pause. Most pauses will have 3–8 tables attempting. Two runners can cover 20 tables.
Round runs over time
8-minute hard cap. If the puzzle isn't solved by Pause 4, emcee reveals the answer and moves on. Drop round 5 if running long overall.
