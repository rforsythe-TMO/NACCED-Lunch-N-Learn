TMO is sponsoring both the keynote and a sponsored lunch at this year's NACCED 51st Annual Educational Conference and Training in Baltimore, MD (October 5–8). NACCED's attendees are predominantly county community development directors and program staff — the exact audience we've been targeting this year for our affordable housing and community development vertical.

Historically, the lunch break has been unstructured. This year, we worked with NACCED to sponsor it and provide food, which gives us a 90-minute block and a captive room. Rather than using it as a second keynote, we designed it as an interactive game show experience — something fun and memorable that the room talks about, while the keynote handles the product education and promotion.

I've been developing two concept options and am attaching two documents for your review:

• The proposal doc — a clean 4-pager written for NACCED that describes both formats and what we'd need from them to execute. This is close to ready to send.
• The internal planning doc (HTML) — a much more detailed internal reference covering game mechanics, section-by-section run of show, question banks, staffing, prep timelines, contingencies, and everything else we'd need to execute either format.

Here's the quick summary of the two options:

Option A — The Funding Feud (Primary)
Inspired by Family Feud. Every table competes simultaneously to guess the most popular answers to survey questions about community development work — questions like "What slows down a good housing project?" or "What does your team rely on to track loan portfolios?" The twist: attendees answer those same questions live via QR code in the first 10 minutes of lunch, so every answer reveal is genuinely "here's what your peers in this room said." We'd also run a pre-event survey of NACCED members and TMO clients to supplement the data and prep the emcee. It closes with a rapid-fire championship round called "Fast Cash for Community" where the top two tables compete for a $1,000 charitable donation in the winning organization's name. We have a steal mechanic, a running leaderboard, and spot prizes throughout.

Option B — Spin the Block (Secondary / Backup)
Inspired by Wheel of Fortune — the name being a play on Community Development Block Grants, which this audience will get immediately. Every table plays every puzzle at the same time on one shared screen. Two wheels drive the game: a big wheel at the front of the room sets a room-wide modifier each round (e.g., "Double Points," "Speed Round," "Blind Round"), and a small branded tabletop spinner at each table sets that table's starting point bank. The core mechanic is simple: the emcee reveals letters one at a time, and tables decide at each pause whether they know enough to solve now and keep more points, or wait for more letters and accept fewer. Field expertise is rewarded — CDBG veterans will recognize phrases faster. Puzzles are drawn from five themed categories (Project Roadblocks, Acronym Soup, Affordable Housing Programs, What High-Performing Counties Do, Fast Track Strategies). It closes with a championship round where the top table takes the stage beside a physical wheel.
